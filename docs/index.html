U.S. Deportations Have Increased 12x in Four Years

More than half a million people were deported from the U.S. between 2020 and 2024 — most without criminal convictions.

By Lara Castelo | June 28, 2026

As a Latin American woman arriving in the United States in 2026, one fear crossed my mind: deportation. Even with a visa and a short-term stay, I wasn’t immune to that anxiety — and I know I’m not alone.

From political speeches to policy shifts and police actions, the United States has sent a clear message to many Latin Americans: you are not welcome.

To understand how that sentiment translates into reality, I turned to data. And, as it turns out, the numbers confirm the feeling.

According to the Department of Homeland Security (DHS), more than half a million people from 44 countries were officially deported from the United States between October 2020 and September 2024.

That’s the equivalent of more than the entire population of Atlanta.

But these are only the recorded figures — the real numbers are likely much higher.

The DHS database only includes dates when more than ten people were deported, meaning smaller-scale removals were not counted.

Who Is Targeted by U.S. Deportations

Data reveals a clear pattern: U.S. deportation policies disproportionately affect Latin American communities, especially those closest to the border, such as Mexicans.

Most Deportees Don't Have Criminal Records

In the United States, people can be deported for reasons ranging from visa overstays to criminal convictions that trigger mandatory removal.

Yet only 35% of people deported during this period had any criminal record. Most were removed for administrative or immigration‑related reasons.
