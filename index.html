99% of U.S. Deportees Over Four Years Came From Latin America

More than half a million people were deported between 2020 and 2024 — most without criminal convictions. Mexico alone accounted for 44 percent.

By Lara Castelo | June 28, 2026

The fear of deportation has become a defining feature of the experience for many Latin Americans arriving in the United States.

And that’s not a coincidence. Political speeches, policy changes and law‑enforcement actions have contributed to a broader message perceived by many: that they are not welcome.

Data helps illustrate how that sentiment plays out in practice. Recent deportation figures show a pattern that aligns closely with these concerns.

Between October 2020 and September 2024, more than half a million people from 44 countries were deported from the U.S., according to data from the U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE).

Ninety‑nine percent of them were from Latin America, with Mexico alone accounting for 44 percent of all removals.

Beyond who is being targeted, the sheer volume of deportations also rose sharply between October 2020 and September 2024.

The total of deportation is almost equivalent to the entire population of Atlanta (Georgia).

But these are only the recorded figures — the real numbers are likely much higher.

The DHS database only includes dates when more than ten people were deported, meaning smaller-scale removals were not counted.

Most Deportees Don't Have Criminal Records

In the United States, people can be deported for reasons ranging from visa overstays to criminal convictions that trigger mandatory removal.

Yet only 35% of people deported during this period had any criminal record. Most were removed for administrative or immigration‑related reasons.
